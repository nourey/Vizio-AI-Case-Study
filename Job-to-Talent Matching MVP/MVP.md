# MVP Design Logic

Using the Talent Schema, I split the MVP problem into 2 sections. First section would called **eligibility filtering**. This section decides whether the applicant should be considered as candidate or not. Second section is to calculating a **fit score** for the candidates that pass the first section.

Before explaining the MVP logic I first need to state and important assumption: for this approach to work effectively the job side must also have the normalized structured fields. That is, I would assume that at least fields like required skills, preferred skills, location or remote type, compensation band and authorization constraints are maintained in a comparable format. 

### First Section: Eligibility Filtering
In first secction I would use **structured eligiblity rules**. For this **candidate_work_authorizations** and **candidate_compensation_expectations** would be the starting point. If the candidate is not authorized to work in job country or have unmatched salary expectations the candidate can be eliminated early. But the filtering should not be this at all. Columns like: remote_preferences, willing_to_relocate, actual location of the candidate and seniority level in some jobs should also considered. For example; if the position requires onsite but the candidate is not willing to relocate, even tough the applicant is powerful in terms of semantically, should not be considered as candidate. So the point of this section is to eliminate candidates that are not eligible clearly. 

### Second Section: Fit Score 
In the second section, fit score is calculated for the remaining candidates. It would not be fully true to calculate this score purely by embedding similarity. In this stage, both structured and semantic fit should be used. On the structured side **candidate_skills** table is valuable. Skills that the candidate has, the proficiency_level on those skills and years_experience, if exists, should be passed into score. Which means that the system should not only look at that "Does candidate know Python?", instead it should consider the knowledge level and how long they have been using it. 

Furthermore, seniority alignment can be calculated by comparing the **candidates.seniority** field with job seniority field. Similarly candidate role preferences is an important indicator; if the candidate's preferred roles are similar to the job's role family, this should be reflected positively in the score. 

I would consider the semantic layer as a second signal that completes the structured match logic. Embedding based similarity can be calculated between a candidate's CV, professional summary or current title and their job description. This similarity is measured using a metric like cosine similarity to determine how semantically close the candidate's experience is to the role. This is especially useful for capturing experiences that are expressed in different words but are actually similar. However the important management decision here is that I keep the wieght of the semantic score limited in the system. That is, the embedding layer alone does not make the decision; the structured layer is the main determinant, while the semantic layer improves the ranking. For example; structured fit might carry higher weight, while semantic fit can be a supporting layer. Also instead of using similarity scores blindly, thresholds or score bands can be defined. Very low similarity can be labeled as a weak match, medium as ambiguous and high as a strong semantic fit. This makes the semantic layer manageable and calibratable. 

Therefore, for me the final fit_score is not a single similarity metric, but a combination of several components. For example, components like authorization match, compensation match, location-remote match, seniority alignment, skill overlap, proficiency strength, role preference alignment, and semantic similarity can be weighted together into a single score. This makes the system more robust and explainsable to the recruiter.

Explainability is very important here. Instead of showing the recruiter only a single score, it is necessary to show the underlying reasons for the score. For example; providing sub explanations such as whether the candidate passed the eligibility filter, whether the work authorization is appropriate, whether the compensation matches, whether the required skill overlap is strong, whether the proficiency is sufficient, how is the seniority alignment, and what level of semantic relevance is available, makes the system more reliable. This is important for the MVP, because in the initial version, user trust is often more valuable than model complexity. Another critical point is missing data management. In real life not every candidate may have complete information on compensation expectation, work authorization, role preference, or skills. In this case, it is not correct for the system to directly use every missing piece of data as a reason for elimination. A more logical approach would be to evaluate any missing critical areas as low confidence or not enough data. In other words, the system should not only have a fit score but also an internal confidence logic. This allows the recruiter to understand the difference between a high score but low confidence and a high score with high confidence.

So according to this schema, my MVP approach would be: first eliminate candidates who are clearly unsuitable using structured eligibility rules, then combine structured skill and preference signals with semantic relevance for the remaining candidates to generate an explainable fit score. The goal is not to build a flawless AI matching engine at first shot; it's to build a fast, logical, manageable, and improvable system. This would be a strong and realistic answer in terms of the case study.
